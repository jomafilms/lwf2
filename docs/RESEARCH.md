## 7. Fire-Specific / Defensible Space Tools

**This is the most critical competitive category for LWF.** Very few dedicated software tools exist here — this is where the biggest market gap lies.

### Fire Aside
- **URL:** https://www.fireaside.com
- **Target user:** Fire agencies, city/county governments, community organizations
- **Key features:** Defensible Space Inspection (DSI) tool; wildfire resilience assessment; data collection; resident communication; vegetation removal tracking; grant management
- **Plant database:** No — focused on assessment, not plant selection
- **Fire data:** Yes — core feature (risk assessment, defensible space evaluation)
- **Zone mapping:** Yes — property-level zone mapping for defensible space
- **Satellite view:** Likely (for property assessment)
- **AI features:** Limited
- **Mobile app:** Field assessment tools (likely mobile-enabled)
- **Pricing:** Enterprise/government — custom pricing
- **Pipeline connection:** No — serves agencies, not the nursery/landscaper/homeowner chain
- **Strengths:** Only dedicated wildfire resilience software platform; trusted by 100+ agencies across 12 states; comprehensive assessment workflow
- **Weaknesses:** Government/agency focused — NOT for homeowners, landscapers, or nurseries; no plant selection or landscape design; no consumer-facing features
- **⭐ KEY COMPETITOR INSIGHT:** Fire Aside is the closest competitor in the fire space, but it serves a completely different user (government agencies) and a completely different use case (inspections/compliance). It does NOT help people design fire-safe landscapes or find fire-resistant plants.

### Planscape
- **URL:** https://www.planscape.org
- **Target user:** Regional planners, forest managers, government agencies
- **Key features:** Wildfire resilience decision support; landscape treatment prioritization; ecological benefit analysis; climate adaptation planning
- **Plant database:** No — landscape-scale treatment planning, not plant-level
- **Fire data:** Yes — extensive (ForSys, FVS, GridFire models; Regional Resource Kits)
- **Zone mapping:** Yes — large-scale landscape zone mapping
- **Satellite view:** Yes — map-based interface
- **AI features:** Limited (modeling/simulation)
- **Mobile app:** Web-based
- **Pricing:** Free — open source (USFS + Google.org + UC collaboration)
- **Pipeline connection:** No
- **Strengths:** Free; backed by USFS + Google; best available fire science data; open source
- **Weaknesses:** Regional/landscape scale (not property level); for professional planners only; no plant selection or landscaping guidance; no consumer UX

### WyldSafe
- **URL:** https://wyldsafe.com
- **Target user:** Homeowners in fire-prone areas
- **Key features:** Wildfire preparedness education; defensible space guidance; fire-resistant plant information; vegetation management tips
- **Plant database:** Educational content about fire-resistant plants (not a searchable database)
- **Fire data:** Yes — educational
- **Zone mapping:** Conceptual zone explanation
- **Satellite view:** No
- **AI features:** No
- **Mobile app:** No — website/content only
- **Pricing:** Free (educational resource)
- **Pipeline connection:** No
- **Strengths:** Accessible homeowner education; fire zone concepts well explained
- **Weaknesses:** Content/education only — not a tool; no interactive features; no design capability

### Calflora Fire-Safe Plant Lists
- **URL:** https://www.calflora.org/app/ipl?list_id=px2154

## 9. Gap Analysis & Strategic Opportunities

### What's MISSING in the Market

#### 🔥 GAP #1: Fire-Safe Landscape DESIGN Tool (THE BIG ONE)
**Nobody combines fire safety data with landscape design.** This is the single biggest gap.
- Fire tools (Fire Aside, Planscape) → assess risk but don't help design landscapes
- Design tools (PRO Landscape, iScape, SketchUp) → have plant libraries but zero fire data
- Fire plant lists (Calflora, Fire Safe Marin) → static PDFs/lists, not integrated into any design workflow
- **LWF's opportunity: Be the FIRST tool that combines a fire-resistant plant database with actual landscape design, zone mapping, and defensible space guidance.**

#### 🔥 GAP #2: Nursery → Landscaper → Homeowner Pipeline
**No software connects the entire chain.** Every tool serves ONE user in the chain:
- Nurseries use: Plantrix, Rapid Garden POS, Lightspeed (inventory/POS)
- Landscapers use: Jobber, LMN, Aspire (business management) + PRO Landscape, DynaSCAPE (design) — separate tools!
- Homeowners use: iScape, Yardzen, ShrubHub (design inspiration/services)
- **Nobody connects nursery plant availability → landscaper design tools → homeowner approval**
- **LWF's opportunity: Build the pipeline. Nursery stocks fire-resistant plants → landscaper designs with available plants → homeowner approves and orders.**

#### 🔥 GAP #3: Climate/Region-Specific Plant Intelligence
Most tools have generic plant databases (zone hardiness at best). None incorporate:
- Fire resistance ratings per plant
- Regional fire zone requirements
- Defensible space zone-appropriate plant placement
- Water usage + fire resistance combined scoring
- Native vs. invasive species flagging for fire contexts
- **LWF's opportunity: Charisse's 1,300+ plant database with fire resistance data is a UNIQUE ASSET that no competitor has.**

#### 🔥 GAP #4: Homeowner Compliance & City/HOA Submission
- Landscapers currently submit plans to cities/HOAs as PDFs or paper plans
- No standardized digital submission workflow exists
- AB 3074 (California) and similar legislation requires defensible space compliance
- No tool helps homeowners or landscapers generate compliant defensible space plans
- **LWF's opportunity: Generate defensible space plans that can be submitted directly for compliance.**

#### 🔥 GAP #5: Accessible Fire-Safe Design for Homeowners
- Most fire-safety information exists as government PDFs and scattered web pages
- Homeowners in fire zones don't know what to plant or where
- No tool lets a homeowner enter their address and get a fire-safe landscape plan
- **LWF's opportunity: "Enter your address → see your fire zones → get a plant plan" would be revolutionary.**

#### 🔥 GAP #6: Mobile-First Fire-Adapted Landscape Tool
- Professional tools are desktop-only (Windows in many cases)
- Fire assessment happens in the field
- No mobile app combines plant identification + fire resistance + zone mapping
- **LWF's opportunity: Mobile-first tool for field use by landscapers AND homeowners.**

### Who Serves the Nursery→Landscaper→Homeowner Funnel?

**Answer: Essentially nobody, comprehensively.**

Closest attempts:
- **iScape** — links to product purchases, but doesn't connect nursery inventory to designs
- **Yardzen/ShrubHub** — provides plant lists and contractor matching, but it's a one-off service
- **DynaSCAPE** — has business management + design, but no nursery or homeowner integration
- **PRO Landscape** — has estimating + design, but no nursery inventory connection

The pipeline today is **completely fragmented:**
1. Homeowner wants a new yard → hires landscaper OR buys plants at nursery randomly
2. Landscaper designs plan → sources plants from nursery via phone/email/visit
3. Nursery has inventory → no way to expose availability to landscapers' design tools
4. Plan approval (if needed) → paper/PDF to city, often rejected, redo cycle

### Is Anyone Combining Fire Safety with Landscape Design?

**No. This is a blue ocean.**

- Fire tools are about assessment/compliance (government-facing)
- Design tools are about aesthetics/functionality (no fire awareness)
- Plant databases have fire data OR design integration, never both
- The closest thing is scattered PDF plant lists from county fire safe councils

### What Are Landscapers Actually Using Day-to-Day?

Based on research, the typical landscaper's tech stack:
1. **Business management:** Jobber or LMN (scheduling, invoicing, CRM)
2. **Design (if they design at all):** PRO Landscape, DynaSCAPE, or just pen-and-paper/iPad sketches
3. **Communication:** Phone, email, text with clients
4. **Plant sourcing:** Phone calls to local nurseries, visiting nurseries in person
5. **Estimating:** Spreadsheets or built-in estimating in Jobber/LMN/DynaSCAPE
6. **Photos:** iPhone camera for before/after and site assessment

**Key insight:** Many landscapers (especially maintenance crews and yard workers) don't use design software at all. They're hands-on, mobile-first, and often working from simple sketches or verbal instructions.

### How Do Landscapers Submit Plans to Cities/HOAs?

Current workflow (manual and painful):
1. Create plan in design software (if used) or by hand
2. Export/print as PDF
3. Email or physically deliver to city planning department or HOA
4. Wait for review (weeks to months)
5. Receive feedback (often rejection or revision requests)
6. Revise and resubmit
7. No standard format — every jurisdiction has different requirements

For defensible space specifically:
- California AB 3074 expanded requirements
- Many WUI (Wildland-Urban Interface) areas require defensible space compliance
- Inspections are done by fire agencies (using tools like Fire Aside)
- No digital pipeline from plan creation → submission → approval → inspection

### 1. **The Market Gap is REAL and MASSIVE**

### 2. **Charisse's Plant Database is the Moat**

### 3. **Three Entry Points to Consider**

Landscapers and homeowners are both mobile-first. Many professional tools are still desktop/Windows-only. A mobile-first approach is differentiated.

Short-term: Serve one user well (probably homeowners or landscapers)
